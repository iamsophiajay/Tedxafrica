Inside the Five-Day Speaker Journey

The talk is sixteen minutes. The preparation is five days. Here's what those days actually look like.

Ask most people what a TEDx talk requires, and they'll describe a single afternoon: arrive, rehearse once, walk on stage. That is not how it works here.

Every speaker accepted into Project TEDxAfrica moves through a structured five-day arc, beginning with onboarding and strategic alignment, where they meet their advisory faculty member and pressure-test the idea they intend to bring to the stage. From there, the days move through idea development, narrative and talk design, and executive rehearsals, each one building on the last.

The final stretch is what we call the Executive Speaker Summit: a gathering in Abuja where every confirmed speaker rehearses live, receives peer feedback, makes final refinements, and has room to simply be in the same space as the other people about to share the stage with them. By the time speakers walk out for their talk, they have already rehearsed it in front of the people who understand exactly what it took to get there.

This is deliberate. A sixteen-minute talk that changes how people think rarely survives on inspiration alone. It survives on structure, feedback, and repetition, applied with enough care that none of it shows on stage.
